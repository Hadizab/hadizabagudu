Weaving Words, Bridging Worlds

I am a Nigerian writer, poet, blogger, podcaster, and entrepreneur based in Abuja. With roots in both creative literature and academic research, I bridge the worlds of artistic expression and scholarly analysis.

About Me

My journey as a storyteller began long before I put pen to paper. Growing up in Northern Nigeria, I was surrounded by rich oral traditions—folktales told under moonlight, proverbs that carried generations of wisdom, and poetry that danced between Hausa, Arabic, and English.

These early influences shaped my desire to capture the human experience in all its complexity. My work explores themes of faith, identity, love, and the delicate threads that connect us across cultures.

Today, I write to bridge worlds—between tradition and modernity, between Nigeria and the global stage, between the spiritual and the everyday. Every word I craft is an invitation to see the world through new eyes.

My Story

In my early years, at primary school, in the 1980s, I stumbled upon an amazing poem about scars. Our teacher, bless her heart, explained how scars aren't just physical marks but reminders of the battles we've fought. That lesson stuck with me, inspiring me to write two poetry collections and four novels since then.

Despite my natural shyness and reluctance to participate in public events like poetry readings and competitions, I've found joy in sharing my work online, thanks to the wonders of technology. My academic journey includes earning a BSc in International Studies from A.B.U Zaria and an MSc in International Relations and Diplomacy from Nile University, Abuja — completing my thesis in 2024 on COVID-19 vaccine nationalism and dependency in Africa.

Today, I'm happily settled in Abuja, Nigeria, with my husband and two children, embracing the balance between family life and my passion for writing and learning.

“Stories are bridges between souls. They allow us to walk in another's shoes, to feel their joys and sorrows, and to recognize ourselves in the unfamiliar.”

A Taste of Nigeria

Stories from my kitchen — where flavor meets memory, and every dish carries a piece of home.

🍗 Nigerian Peppered Chicken

Deeply spiced, crispy fried chicken tossed in a rich, fragrant pepper sauce.

⏱️ Prep: 20 mins 🍲 Cook: 25 mins 👥 Serves: 6-8 🌶️ Heat: Bold
🌿 View Ingredients
  • 1 whole chicken (cut into pieces)
  • 1 large or 2 medium bell peppers
  • 4 attarugu (scotch bonnet) or hot peppers
  • 1 tomato, 1 large onion
  • Salt, curry powder, thyme, chicken seasoning
  • 1 double Knorr chicken cube and 1 Royco cube
  • Oil for frying
📖 View Method
  1. Boil the Chicken: Cut chicken into pieces, wash, and place in a pot with 1 tsp salt, ½ tsp curry, chicken seasoning, Royco cube, thyme, and half the chopped onion. Cover and cook for 30 mins until soft.
  2. Fry the Chicken: Remove chicken pieces and deep-fry in hot oil until golden brown. Set aside.
  3. Prepare the Pepper Sauce: Blend remaining onion, bell peppers, hot peppers, tomato, and garlic. Cook mixture until water dries up, then add oil and fry until oil separates.
  4. Combine and Finish: Season sauce with ½ tsp salt, curry, chicken seasoning, thyme, and 1 Knorr cube. Fry 30 seconds, then add fried chicken. Mix well and fry another 30 seconds.

✨ Serve hot with jollof rice, fried rice, boiled yam, or plantain.

👨🏾‍🍳 Chef's Tips: The 30+30 seconds rule keeps chicken crispy. Add smoked paprika for extra smokiness. Reduce scotch bonnets to 2 for milder heat.

🥣 Eba & Okro Soup

Smooth, golden eba paired with rich, slimy okra soup — a wholesome taste of home.

⏱️ Prep: 20 mins 🍲 Cook: 55 mins 👥 Serves: 6-8
🌿 View Ingredients
  • 500g beef (cut into chunks)
  • 2 cups okra (chopped)
  • 1 onion (chopped)
  • 2 scotch bonnet peppers
  • 1 bell pepper, 1 tomato
  • 1 tsp iru (locust beans)
  • 1 tsp liquid potash (akanwu)
  • Seasoning cubes, salt, curry, thyme
  • 2 cups garri (for eba)
📖 View Method

Okro Soup: Boil beef with onion, garlic, salt, stock cube, curry, thyme. Blend onion, bell pepper, tomatoes, scotch bonnet. Fry until oil separates. Add water, salt, stock cubes, iru. Boil 5 mins. Add chopped okra, cook 5 mins. Add liquid potash, cook 5 mins. Add beef and stock.

Eba: Boil 2 cups water. Slowly sprinkle garri while stirring until thick. Cover on low heat 5 mins. Stir vigorously, cover, rest 10 mins. Shape and serve.

✨ Serve warm, with the soup generously ladled over the eba.

👨🏾‍🍳 Chef's Tips: Liquid potash (akanwu) gives okro soup its signature texture. For smoother eba, stir vigorously in one direction and let it rest covered.

🍲 Semo & Egusi Soup

A classic Nigerian comfort meal — rich melon seed stew with semolina.

⏱️ Prep: 20 mins 🍲 Cook: 70 mins 👥 Serves: 6-8
🌿 View Ingredients
  • 2 cups egusi (melon seeds, ground)
  • 500g beef or assorted meat
  • 1 cup smoked fish or stockfish
  • 2 cups pumpkin leaves (ugwu) or spinach
  • 1 onion, 2 scotch bonnet peppers
  • 1 bell pepper, 2 tomatoes
  • ½ cup palm oil
  • 2 tbsp crayfish (ground)
  • Seasoning cubes, salt
  • 2 cups semolina flour (for semo)
📖 View Method

Egusi Soup: Boil beef with salt and Maggi. Mix egusi with crayfish into paste. Fry garlic, onions in oil. Add blended peppers, fry until soft. Add beef, crayfish, daddawa, seasonings. Add fish. Scoop egusi paste, cook 7 mins, stir, cook 5 more mins. Add remaining palm oil, ugwu, water leaves, cook 5 mins.

Semolina: Boil water. Mix semo with cold water into paste. Pour into boiling water, cook 5 mins (careful — it rises!). Slowly add semolina while stirring until thick. Cook 5 mins, turn off heat, let finish on hot stove.

👨🏾‍🍳 Chef's Tips: Add smoked fish for richer flavor. Adjust water for consistency. Some prefer egusi in lumps, others broken — both are delicious.

🍚 Nigerian Party Jollof Rice

The legendary one-pot party rice — smoky, vibrant, unforgettable.

⏱️ Prep: 20 mins 🍲 Cook: 50 mins 👥 Serves: 6-8
🌿 View Ingredients
  • 4 cups long-grain rice (rinsed)
  • 800g fresh tomato blend + 2 tbsp tomato paste
  • 2 large onions (diced)
  • 3-4 scotch bonnet peppers
  • ½ cup vegetable oil
  • 2 tbsp thyme, 2 tbsp curry powder
  • 3 seasoning cubes & salt to taste
  • 4 cups chicken stock
  • 1 tsp garlic powder, 1 tsp ginger powder
  • 2 bay leaves
📖 View Method
  1. Blend tomatoes, scotch bonnet into smooth puree. Fry in hot oil until reduced and deep red.
  2. Add diced onions, garlic, ginger, fry 3 mins. Add tomato paste, curry, thyme, seasoning cubes.
  3. Pour in chicken stock and bring to a boil. Add bay leaves and rinsed rice.
  4. Cover and cook on low heat until rice absorbs liquid (~25-30 mins).

✨ For smoky flavor, let it burn slightly at bottom. Serve with fried plantain.

👨🏾‍🍳 Chef's Tips: Add 1 tsp Nigerian curry powder and ½ tsp white pepper for deeper warmth. Leftover Jollof fried with onions makes 'Jollof rice stir-fry' — even better the next day!

The Architecture of Nigerian Fantasy

March 15, 2025 • 8 min read

Writing Craft Fantasy

When I wrote Fantah, I didn't set out to create a fantasy novel. I set out to tell a story about a Bororo girl finding her place in the world. But as the characters grew, so did the world around them — and I realized I was building something entirely new.

The Foundation: Oral Traditions

Growing up in Northern Nigeria, I was surrounded by stories told under moonlight. My grandmother would weave tales of spirits in the baobab trees, of heroes who crossed rivers on the backs of crocodiles, of love that spanned villages and centuries. These weren't just bedtime stories — they were my first encounter with worldbuilding.

Bridging Worlds

In Fantah and the Astral Series, I wanted to honor those oral traditions while creating something accessible to global readers. The kingdoms in Fantah are inspired by real Nigerian landscapes — the rolling hills of Zuma Rock, the ancient city of Kano, the mystical forests of the Niger Delta.

“Fantah transported me to another world. Bagudu's storytelling is rich, immersive, and utterly unforgettable.” — Goodreads Reviewer

What's Next for Nigerian Fantasy

I believe we're at the beginning of a renaissance in African fantasy literature. Writers like Nnedi Okorafor, Tomi Adeyemi, and others have opened doors. My hope is that Fantah and the Astral series add another brick to that beautiful architecture.

What stories from your childhood would you like to see reimagined as fantasy? Share with me in the comments below or on social media.

The Hajj Journey That Changed Me

March 28, 2025 • 10 min read

Faith & Spirituality Memoir

In 2019, I stood on the plains of Arafat surrounded by millions of souls from every corner of the earth. We spoke different languages, wore different clothes, carried different stories — but in that moment, we were the same. We were pilgrims seeking closeness to the Divine.

That journey became the seed for “An Epic Journey of Faith.” But more than a book, it became a lens through which I now see all my writing.

The Power of Shared Sacred Space

The Hajj taught me that the most powerful stories are those that remind us of our shared humanity. When I write — whether it's a poem about love or a novel about a prince seeking redemption — I'm always trying to create sacred space on the page.

“An Extraordinary Experience is exactly that. A faith-filled journey that touched my heart.” — Amazon Customer

Have you ever had a journey — physical or spiritual — that changed your perspective? I'd love to hear about it.

Why I Write About Scars

March 10, 2025 • 4 min read

Poetry Inspiration

In my early years, at primary school in the 1980s, I stumbled upon an amazing poem about scars. Our teacher, bless her heart, explained how scars aren't just physical marks but reminders of the battles we've fought.

That lesson stuck with me. It inspired me to write two poetry collections and four novels. Because every character I create — Fantah, Hayah, Melody, Yusuf — carries scars. Not just the kind you can see, but the invisible ones we all bear.

Scars as Story

When I wrote Ripples, I thought about how every scar tells a story of survival. The collection became a meditation on becoming — moving from water to fire to simply being here.

“Ripples is a soul-stirring collection where each poem casts a stone into the still waters of memory.”

Bororo Girl to Published Author

February 20, 2025 • 7 min read

Culture & Identity Memoir

Fantah, the determined Bororo girl at the heart of my novel, shares more with me than readers might realize. Her journey — from the grasslands of Northern Nigeria to discovering her place in a complex world — mirrors my own.

Like Fantah, I grew up surrounded by traditions that felt both anchoring and limiting. I learned early that being a girl meant certain expectations. But I also learned, from the strong women around me, that expectations could be rewritten.

Finding My Voice in Multiple Languages

Today, I speak 3 languages fluently: English, Hausa, and Fulfulde. I can also read and write Arabic. Each language carries a different part of my identity. When I write, I'm not just choosing English because it's accessible — I'm translating entire worlds between the languages that shaped me.

“The compelling journey of Fantah takes readers on an unforgettable expedition through diverse landscapes and intriguing kingdoms.”

To every young girl from a small town with big dreams: your voice matters. Keep writing. Keep becoming.

The Making of the Astral Series

February 5, 2025 • 9 min read

Behind the Books Fantasy

Yusuf's story began with a question: What if the space between sleep and wakefulness was actually a doorway?

I've always been fascinated by dreams — the way they can feel more real than waking life. In many Nigerian traditions, dreams are considered portals, messages from ancestors or glimpses into other dimensions. The Astral Series became my way of exploring that belief.

From Concept to Completion

Writing the Astral Series took me three years. I researched astral projection, traveled to learn from traditional spiritual guides, and spent countless nights journaling my own dreams. Yusuf's journey became my journey.

“Embark on a cosmic odyssey where boundaries between worlds dissolve. Join Yusuf as he discovers the ancient art of astral projection and unlocks the secrets of inter-dimensional travel.”

The Astral Series: Book One is available now. Have you ever had a dream that felt like a doorway to another world?
